Legal Perspective on AI 
Regulations, Guidance and Technical Accountability

1. Ensuring AI is used Responsibly: https://www.dhs.gov/ai/ensuring-ai-is-used-responsibly
* The Department of Homeland Security (DHS) states that its use of artificial intelligence must be lawful, mission-appropriate, safe, secure, responsible, trustworthy, and human-centered.
* DHS uses a combination of principles, policies, governance, and collaboration across the department to ensure AI is used responsibly and supports homeland security missions.
* Responsible AI use at DHS includes protecting privacy, civil rights, and civil liberties, and ensuring AI systems do not create unfair bias or discrimination.
* DHS has established governance structures, policies, and oversight frameworks to manage risks, monitor AI systems, and ensure accountability in AI development and use.
* The department also focuses on risk management, testing, and evaluation of AI systems to ensure they are reliable, secure, and perform as intended before and after deployment.
* DHS promotes transparency, workforce training, and collaboration to improve responsible AI implementation across its programs and operations.


2. ENSURING A NATIONAL POLICY FRAMEWORK FOR ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE: https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/12/eliminating-state-law-obstruction-of-national-artificial-intelligence-policy/ 
   1. The presidential action aims to prevent state laws from interfering with national AI policy and innovation, emphasizing the need for a unified federal approach to AI regulation.
   2. It argues that inconsistent state AI laws could slow innovation, increase compliance costs, and weaken U.S. global competitiveness in artificial intelligence.
   3. The policy emphasizes that AI development is important for national security, economic growth, and technological leadership.
   4. Federal agencies are directed to review state AI regulations and identify laws that may conflict with federal objectives.
   5. The action supports innovation-friendly regulation while still addressing risks such as privacy, safety, and civil rights protections.
   6. The overall goal is to create a consistent national framework for AI governance rather than a patchwork of state-level regulations.
3. Regulating Artificial Intelligence: U.S. and International Approaches and Considerations for Congress: https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48555 
   1. The report provides an overview of U.S. artificial intelligence policy, governance, and legislative issues.
   2. It explains that AI policy in the U.S. focuses on innovation, national security, economic competitiveness, and risk management.
   3. Congress is considering issues such as privacy, bias and discrimination, transparency, safety, workforce impacts, and intellectual property.
   4. The report describes how multiple federal agencies are involved in AI regulation and oversight, rather than a single AI regulator.
   5. It discusses federal investments in AI research and development to maintain U.S. leadership in AI technology.
   6. The report notes that policymakers are trying to balance innovation with regulation and risk mitigation.
   7. International competition, particularly with other countries investing heavily in AI, is a major driver of U.S. AI policy.


4. European Parliament – EU AI Act Overview: https://www.europarl.europa.eu/topics/en/article/20230601STO93804/eu-ai-act-first-regulation-on-artificial-intelligence
   1. The EU AI Act creates a risk-based regulatory framework for artificial intelligence in the European Union.
   2. AI systems are classified into four risk categories: unacceptable risk, high risk, limited risk, and minimal risk.
   3. Unacceptable risk AI systems (such as certain forms of social scoring or manipulative AI) are banned.
   4. High-risk AI systems (such as those used in hiring, law enforcement, education, and critical infrastructure) must meet strict requirements for safety, transparency, and human oversight.
   5. The law requires transparency for AI systems like chatbots and deepfakes, so users know when they are interacting with AI.
   6. The EU AI Act is designed to protect fundamental rights, safety, and privacy while still supporting innovation.
   7. The Act is considered one of the world’s first comprehensive AI regulatory frameworks.
 
5. EU Artificial Intelligence Act: https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/ 
   1. The EU Artificial Intelligence Act is a European Union regulation that creates a comprehensive legal framework for artificial intelligence, establishing rules for how AI systems are developed, used, and monitored in the EU.
   2. The Act uses a risk-based approach, classifying AI systems based on the level of risk they pose, including unacceptable risk, high risk, limited risk, and minimal risk.
   3. Unacceptable risk AI systems are banned, while high-risk systems are allowed but must meet strict legal requirements related to safety, transparency, and oversight.
   4. The regulation applies to organizations that develop, sell, import, or use AI systems in the EU, even if the organization is located outside the European Union.
   5. The Act also includes transparency requirements for certain AI systems, meaning users must be informed when they are interacting with AI or AI-generated content.
   6. The overall goal of the AI Act is to ensure AI systems in the EU are safe, respect human rights, and protect health, safety, and the environment, while still supporting the use and development of AI.
   7. The EU AI Act is considered the first comprehensive AI law by a major regulator, and it establishes a unified framework for AI regulation across EU member states.


6. https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai
   1. The EU Artificial Intelligence Act is the first comprehensive legal framework on artificial intelligence, designed to address AI risks while positioning Europe as a global leader in AI.
   2. The framework aims to promote trustworthy, human-centric AI while ensuring safety, fundamental rights, and innovation across the European Union.
   3. The AI Act uses a risk-based approach, meaning AI systems are regulated based on the level of risk they pose, with stricter requirements for higher-risk systems.
   4. The AI Act is part of a broader EU AI strategy that includes innovation programs, investment initiatives, and support for AI development and adoption across Europe.
   5. The European Commission created initiatives such as the AI Pact and AI Act Service Desk to help organizations prepare for and implement the new AI rules.
   6. The purpose of the regulatory framework is to ensure AI systems are safe and trustworthy while supporting innovation, investment, and economic growth in AI across the EU.
